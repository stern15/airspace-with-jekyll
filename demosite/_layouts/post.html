by Jana Bergant

Posted on January 08,2016

If there is a lesson to be learned, it is the futility of seeking fulfillment in outer space. we need to judge ourselfs by who we are, not by where we go.

Never in all their history have men been able truly to conceive of the world as one: a single sphere, a globe, having the qualities of a globe, a round earth in which all directions eventually meet,in which all men occupy as equals. The airman's earth, if free men make it, will be truly aound: a globe in practice, not in theory.

Science cuts two ways, of course; its products can be used for both good and evil. But there's no turning back from science. The early warnings about technological dangers also come form science.

What was most significant about the lunar voyage was not that man set foot on the Moon but that set eye on the earth.

A chinese tale tells of some men sent to harm a young gitl who, upon seeing her beauty, become her protectors rather than her violators. That's how I felt seeing the Earth for the first time. I could not help but love and cherish her.

For those who have seen the earth form space, and for the hundreds and perhaps thousands more > who will, the experience most certainly changes your perspective. The things that we share in > our world are far more valuable than those which divide us.

The final Frontier

There can be no thought of finishing for 'aiming for the stars.' Both figuratively and literally, it is a task to occupy the generations. And no matters how much progress one makes, there is always the thrill of just beginning.

The dreams of the yesterday are the hopes of today and the reality of tommorrow. Science has not > > yet mastered prophecy. We predict too much for the next year and yet far too little for the > > next ten.

Spaceflight cannot be stopped. This is not the work of any one man or even a group pf men. It is a historical process which mankind is carrying out in accordance with the natural laws of human development.
